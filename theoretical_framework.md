# Theoretical Framework

**Making Taste Legible: Symbolic Boundaries and Expert Valuation in Whisky Reviews**

*Reference document for writing the paper's theory section. This file provides the conceptual architecture, key theorists, usable framings, and the bridge between theory and computational methodology. It is not the paper itself; it is the scaffolding.*

## 1. Central thesis

> Expert whisky reviews translate private sensory experience into public cultural value by drawing symbolic boundaries — between naturalness and artificiality, complexity and simplicity, balance and disorder — that are measurable as structured dimensions in the semantic space of evaluative language.

Every section of the paper should support and elaborate this sentence.

## 2. The translation chain

Expert tasting notes are neither chemical analysis nor subjective emotion. They perform a translation process with four stages:

```
Chemical sensation  →  Sensory perception  →  Cultural analogy  →  Evaluative judgment
```

### Worked example: "tropical fruit"

1. **Chemical sensation:** The reviewer's nose detects specific volatile esters and aromatic compounds (e.g. ethyl butyrate, isoamyl acetate).
2. **Sensory perception:** These compounds are perceived as a "fruity" quality — a broad perceptual category.
3. **Cultural analogy:** The reviewer selects "tropical fruit" (mango, passion fruit, pineapple) rather than "orchard fruit" (apple, pear). This choice is not neutral. "Tropical" connotes exoticism, richness, maturity, and rarity within the whisky evaluative repertoire. "Apple" connotes ordinariness.
4. **Evaluative judgment:** The presence of "tropical fruit" descriptors is associated with higher scores. The analogy carries evaluative weight.

### Worked example: "rubber"

1. **Chemical sensation:** Specific sulphur compounds or mercaptans.
2. **Sensory perception:** An acrid, chemically harsh quality.
3. **Cultural analogy:** The reviewer writes "rubber" — not just "sharp" or "chemical." "Rubber" places the whisky in a symbolic domain of industrial manufacture, artificiality, and contamination.
4. **Evaluative judgment:** The descriptor signals that something is wrong — the whisky has crossed a symbolic boundary from legitimate maturation into defect territory.

### Worked example: "farmyard"

This case illustrates how the same sensory quality can fall on either side of a symbolic boundary:

- **Positive framing:** "lovely farmyard character — hay, earth, moss, a rural honesty" → connotes terroir, organic authenticity, a connection to land and place. Associated with positive or neutral evaluation.
- **Negative framing:** "a bit too farmy — dirty, barnyard, unclean" → connotes uncleanliness, excess, lack of refinement. Associated with negative evaluation.

The difference is not chemical. It is a judgment about where the descriptor falls relative to a culturally defined boundary between "legitimate organic character" and "uncleanliness."

### The key insight

The translation chain shows that what experts describe as sensory qualities are actually culturally structured classifications. The vocabulary does three things simultaneously:

1. **Enables perception** (Hennion) — terms like "waxy" or "rancio" direct attention to specific qualities that untrained tasters would miss.
2. **Classifies evaluatively** (Lamont) — each descriptor places the whisky on one side of a symbolic boundary.
3. **Makes singular experience communicable** (Karpik) — the vocabulary turns a private tasting into a public, comparable evaluation.

## 3. Theory section structure: four moves

### Move 1: Expert taste as practice

**Setup with Bourdieu, then extend with Hennion.**

Bourdieu (1984) argued that aesthetic taste is socially structured: judgments of quality encode and reproduce social hierarchies. In his framework, expert tasting vocabulary is a form of *embodied cultural capital* — a trained perceptual apparatus that classifies through "schemes of perception and appreciation." The vocabulary signals who belongs to the world of connoisseurship and who does not.

However, a purely Bourdieusian reading risks reducing expert language to strategic performance — taste as a badge of distinction. Hennion (2004, 2007) offers a complementary and, for this project, more useful perspective: taste is an *active, embodied, reflexive practice*, not merely a passive reception of stimuli or a social positioning device. The expert taster does not simply "have" preferences; they actively engage — nosing, swirling, comparing, resting, re-tasting — in a practice of *attachment* to the object.

**Why Hennion matters for this project:**

Serge Valentin is independently wealthy and not profit-motivated. His site is a passion project. The Bourdieusian reading (taste as social strategy) does not fully explain his behavior. But Hennion's reading does: Serge's elaborate vocabulary is not ornamental. It is a *technology of attention* — a set of learned perceptual tools that make fine-grained sensory distinctions possible.

**Usable framing:**

> Expert taste is not passive preference. It is an active practice of attention, comparison, classification, and justification. The specialized vocabulary — terms such as "waxy," "rancio," "mineral," or "farmy" — does not merely decorate the review. It enables perception: making fine-grained sensory distinctions perceptible, communicable, and evaluable.

**Connection to methodology:** The domain dictionary (Layer 1) reconstructs the vocabulary through which expert attention is organized. The 9 dictionary categories are not arbitrary word lists; they represent the perceptual-evaluative infrastructure of expertise.

---

### Move 2: Tasting notes as sensory translation (cultural repertoires)

**Ground in Swidler's "culture as toolkit" (1986).**

When Serge writes "tropical fruit" rather than "fruity," or "old books" rather than "musty," he is selecting from a culturally available *repertoire* of sensory analogies. Swidler (1986) argued that culture provides a "toolkit" of symbols, stories, and strategies from which actors construct lines of action. In the tasting context, the toolkit is a repertoire of culturally recognizable objects that the reviewer maps onto sensory experience.

The repertoire is not neutral. It is structured by evaluative associations:

- **Positive domain:** tropical fruit, honey, beeswax, old books, leather, herbs, heather, mineral, orchard blossoms — objects from nature, craft, organic richness, maturity.
- **Negative domain:** rubber, plastic, cardboard, solvent, metallic, rotten cabbage — objects from industrial manufacture, decay, contamination, artificiality.

The selection of analogy does evaluative work. "Tropical fruit" does not merely denote fruitiness; it connotes exotic richness, maturity, and rarity. "Rubber" does not merely denote a chemical compound; it connotes industrial contamination.

**Usable framing:**

> Expert tasting notes draw on a culturally structured repertoire of sensory analogies. The reviewer does not simply report perception; they select, from a learned vocabulary of culturally available objects, those analogies that most effectively communicate the evaluative character of the experience.

**Connection to methodology:** The embedding dimensions (Layer 2) test whether this repertoire has geometric structure. If positive analogies systematically cluster in one region of semantic space and negative analogies in another, the cultural structure of the repertoire is empirically recoverable.

### Move 3: Evaluation as symbolic boundary-making

**Primary frame: Lamont & Molnar (2002). Support: Douglas (1966).**

Lamont and Molnar define *symbolic boundaries* as the conceptual distinctions that social actors draw to categorize objects, people, and practices. Expert whisky evaluation draws symbolic boundaries between:

- Valued and devalued sensory qualities
- Natural/authentic and artificial/industrial qualities
- Complex/balanced and simple/disjointed qualities
- Organic richness and contamination/decay
- Legitimate maturation and defective or forced production

These are not merely sensory distinctions. They are cultural classifications that define what whisky *should be*.

**Douglas for defects:** Mary Douglas (1966) argued that "dirt is matter out of place" — what counts as contamination is culturally defined, not objectively given. A "defect" in whisky is not simply a chemical fact. When a reviewer writes "rubber" or "plastic," the descriptor is negative not because rubber is materially present (it is an analogy for certain sulphur compounds), but because it places the whisky on the wrong side of a symbolic boundary — closer to industrial contamination than to legitimate maturation.

**Key evidence for this claim:** Context-dependent terms. The same sensory quality can be valued or devalued depending on framing:


| Descriptor | Valued framing                               | Devalued framing                    |
| ---------- | -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Smoke      | "beautiful peat smoke, Islay character"      | "acrid, ashy, overwhelming"         |
| Sulphur    | "a whiff, common in young sherry casks"      | "heavy sulphur, ruining the spirit" |
| Farmyard   | "lovely rural character, organic, authentic" | "dirty, barnyard, unclean"          |
| Oak        | "beautiful old sherry oak, mature"           | "over-oaked, planky, too woody"     |


These cases demonstrate that defect classification is cultural, not merely chemical. The boundary between "character" and "defect" is socially constructed.

**Usable framing:**

> Expert descriptors draw boundaries between what properly belongs in whisky and what appears as contamination, excess, artificiality, or defect. A term such as "rubber" is not negative simply because rubber is materially absent from whisky; it is negative because it places the whisky on the wrong side of a symbolic boundary, closer to industrial contamination than to legitimate maturation.

**Connection to methodology:** The embedding dimension Natural-Authentic / Artificial-Industrial directly measures this boundary. WEAT tests check whether high-score descriptors and low-score descriptors are differentially associated with the cultural poles of this boundary.

---

### Move 4: Scores and review format as judgment devices

**Primary theorist: Karpik (2010).**

Karpik argues that for "singular" goods — objects whose quality cannot be assessed through standard market mechanisms (wine, art, restaurants, whisky) — *judgment devices* are required to reduce uncertainty. Guides, critics, rankings, and structured review formats are such devices. They make the ineffable (subjective aesthetic experience) commensurable and comparable.

The Whiskyfun review format is a judgment device:

```
Colour → Nose → Mouth → Finish → Comments → SGP → Score
```

This format is not a mere formatting convention. It *stages* sensory attention: it directs the reviewer to attend systematically to distinct aspects of the experience. It organizes description into publicly recognizable categories. And it culminates in a numerical score that makes singular tasting experiences comparable.

**Why this matters for the project:**

If Nose/Mouth/Finish sections contain score-relevant information even without Comments, then valuation is embedded in the sensory description itself — not merely announced in the explicit evaluative summary. The review structure does not just record evaluation; it structures it.

**Usable framing:**

> The Nose/Mouth/Finish/Comments structure is a judgment device: it stages sensory attention, organizes description, and makes singular aesthetic experiences comparable through numerical scores. Valuation is not merely stated in the Comments; it is already embedded in the structured sensory description that precedes the explicit judgment.

**Connection to methodology:** Section-level analysis tests this directly. If dictionary measures computed on NMF (Nose+Mouth+Finish only) retain predictive signal, the judgment device is doing more than providing a template — it is converting sensation into evaluation at each stage.

## 5. Theory-to-method bridge

This is the most important transitional paragraph in the paper. It connects the theoretical framework to the computational methodology.

> The theoretical framework predicts that expert evaluative language should be organized along symbolic boundaries: natural versus artificial, complex versus simple, balanced versus disjointed. If these boundaries are genuine features of the discourse — rather than categories imposed externally by the analyst — they should be recoverable from the corpus's own semantic geometry. Word embedding dimensions provide exactly this test: they measure whether the relational structure of words in context corresponds to the evaluative axes that the theory predicts. A domain dictionary reconstructs the explicit categories of expert vocabulary; embedding dimensions test whether those categories have continuous geometric structure; and validation checks whether the measures behave as the theory expects when applied to known whisky categories, association tests, and numerical scores.

## 6. How each theorist is used

### Pierre Bourdieu (1984) — *Distinction*

**Role in paper:** Setup. Frames the problem.

**Use:** Bourdieu establishes that aesthetic taste is not merely individual preference but is socially structured through "schemes of perception and appreciation." Expert tasting vocabulary is a form of embodied cultural capital.

**Limitation acknowledged:** A purely Bourdieusian reading reduces taste to social strategy and distinction-signaling. Hennion's corrective is needed.

**Where it appears:** Opening of Theory section (Move 1), first 1-2 paragraphs.

---

### Antoine Hennion (2004, 2007) — Pragmatic sociology of taste

**Role in paper:** Main theoretical frame for understanding *what expert vocabulary does*.

**Use:** Taste is an active, embodied practice. The specialized vocabulary is a technology of attention, not merely a status marker. This explains why expert language is so elaborate: it does perceptual work, not just social work.

**Key text:** Hennion, A. (2004). Pragmatics of taste. In M. Jacobs & N. Hanrahan (Eds.), *The Blackwell Companion to the Sociology of Culture* (pp. 131-144).

**Where it appears:** Move 1 (main frame); connection to dictionary as perceptual infrastructure.

---

### Ann Swidler (1986) — Culture in action

**Role in paper:** Grounds "sensory translation" in established cultural sociology.

**Use:** Experts draw from a culturally structured *toolkit* (repertoire) of sensory analogies. The repertoire is not neutral; it is organized into valued and devalued symbolic domains.

**Key text:** Swidler, A. (1986). Culture in action: Symbols and strategies. *American Sociological Review, 51*(2), 273-286.

**Where it appears:** Move 2; connection to embedding dimensions (repertoire has geometric structure).

---

### Michele Lamont — Symbolic boundaries

**Role in paper:** Primary theoretical frame for the main sociological claim (RQ2).

**Use:** Expert evaluation draws symbolic boundaries between valued and devalued qualities. The boundaries correspond to cultural categories (natural/artificial, complex/simple, balanced/disordered), not just to sensory properties.

**Key text:** Lamont, M., & Molnar, V. (2002). The study of boundaries in the social sciences. *Annual Review of Sociology, 28*, 167-195.

**Also useful:** Lamont, M. (2012). Toward a comparative sociology of valuation and evaluation. *Annual Review of Sociology, 38*, 201-221.

**Where it appears:** Move 3 (primary); RQ2 framing; interpretation of embedding dimensions.

---

### Mary Douglas (1966) — *Purity and Danger*

**Role in paper:** Support for Claim 3 (defects are culturally organized).

**Use:** "Dirt is matter out of place." A whisky defect is not a chemical fact; it is a culturally classified violation. "Rubber" is negative because it places the whisky on the wrong side of a symbolic boundary. The same sensory quality (smoke, sulphur, farmyard) can be valued or devalued depending on context — proof that the classification is cultural.

**Key text:** Douglas, M. (1966). *Purity and Danger: An Analysis of Concepts of Pollution and Taboo*. Routledge.

**Where it appears:** Move 3 (supporting); close-reading interpretation; Discussion.

---

### Lucien Karpik (2010) — *Valuing the Unique*

**Role in paper:** Frames the review format as a judgment device (Claim 4).

**Use:** For singular goods, quality cannot be determined by standard market mechanisms. Judgment devices (guides, critics, structured reviews) reduce uncertainty. The Whiskyfun format stages sensory attention and makes singular experiences comparable.

**Key text:** Karpik, L. (2010). *Valuing the Unique: The Economics of Singularities*. Princeton University Press.

**Where it appears:** Move 4; section-level analysis interpretation; Claim 4.

---

### Svetlana Boym (2001) — *The Future of Nostalgia*

**Role in paper:** Light use, mainly in close reading and Discussion.

**Use:** Distinguishes restorative nostalgia (wants to rebuild the past) from reflective nostalgia (aestheticizes the feeling of loss). Serge's "old bottle effect" and "good old days" references are reflective nostalgia — an aesthetic stance, not a factual claim. This makes the nostalgia in his writing an evaluative aesthetic preference for temporal authenticity.

**Where it appears:** Close reading; Discussion section only. Not a main embedding dimension (terms too sparse).

**Usable sentence:**

> Nostalgic descriptors do not necessarily claim that older production was objectively superior. They mark an aesthetic stance in which temporal distance, rarity, and perceived loss become sources of value.

---

### Methodological theorists

**Grimmer, Roberts & Stewart (2022)** — *Text as Data*. Frames the overall methodology: text methods for discovery, measurement, and validation, not merely prediction.

**Kozlowski, Taddy & Evans (2019)** — "The Geometry of Culture." Direct methodological inspiration for embedding dimensions as cultural dimensions. Cultural categories have continuous geometric structure in semantic space.

**Caliskan, Bryson & Narayanan (2017)** — WEAT tests. Method for validating whether discovered associations align with expected evaluative poles.

**Rodriguez & Spirling (2022)** — Auditing embeddings. Emphasizes that embeddings used as substantive evidence require careful validation of choices (window size, dimensions, local vs. pretrained).

**Nelson (2020)** — Computational grounded theory. Three-step process (pattern detection, refinement, confirmation) maps onto the project's three layers.

## 9. Storytelling hook (introduction opening)

The paper should open with the "not literal ingredients" insight. This is what makes non-whisky readers care: the paper is about how any expert translates private experience into public cultural value.

**Draft opening:**

> When a whisky critic writes that a spirit tastes of "tropical fruit," "old books," "burnt plastic," or "rotten cabbage," the claim is not literal. These objects are not in the glass. Rather, the critic translates sensory perception — the detection of specific volatile compounds — into culturally recognizable analogies. This translation is not neutral. "Tropical fruit" does not merely denote fruitiness; it connotes exotic richness, maturity, and rarity. "Burnt plastic" does not merely denote a chemical compound; it connotes artificiality, industrial contamination, and defect. Expert evaluation, we argue, operates through a systematic mapping of sensory perception onto culturally meaningful categories of value and disvalue.

> This paper uses a corpus of over 11,000 expert whisky reviews to study how specialized evaluative language converts private sensation into public, legitimate, and numerically scored judgments of quality. We treat tasting notes as a case for understanding a broader phenomenon: how experts in evaluative domains — wine critics, restaurant reviewers, art critics — use culturally structured vocabularies to make taste legible.

**Why this works:** It promises generalizability. The paper is about whisky, but the claim extends to any domain where experts perform evaluative translation. This is what makes it computational social science rather than a whisky hobby project.

## 11. Broader contribution — the "so what"

The paper should make clear that whisky is the empirical case, not the ultimate object of interest.

**For text-as-data methodology:** Generic NLP tools (sentiment analysis) systematically miss how domain-specific evaluation works. Domain-specific measurement instruments are needed.

**For cultural sociology:** Expert evaluation is organized not by a simple good/bad axis but by culturally structured symbolic boundaries (nature/artificiality, complexity/simplicity, balance/disorder).

**For the sociology of valuation:** Quality is not an objective property of the object. It is constructed through the cultural categories available to the evaluator and made legible through judgment devices.

**The broad contribution in one sentence:**

> This project shows how culture turns sensation into value.
